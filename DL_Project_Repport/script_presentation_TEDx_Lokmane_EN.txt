SLIDE 4 — Why It's Hard
----------------------------------------------------------------

Slide Title : "Three enemies. One model."

Visual : Three icons:
         [A] Medical vocabulary · [#] Different templates · [Phone] Blurry photos

Script :
  Understanding a prescription means solving three problems at once.

  First enemy: vocabulary.
  "Hydrochlorothiazide" — no classic OCR engine knows this word.
  It's out-of-distribution.

  Second enemy: layout.
  Every clinic uses a different format. Every doctor, their own logic.

  Third enemy: image quality.
  A photo taken with a phone, shadows on the paper, tilted angle…

  [Pause]

  Tesseract, EasyOCR, PaddleOCR — all fail on at least two of these three.

  Our model attacks all three at once.

SLIDE 6 — Act 2: The Solution (Pipeline)
----------------------------------------------------------------

Slide Title : "From raw image to structured JSON — in under 2 seconds."

Visual : Horizontal pipeline:
         Image → Preprocessing → Line Segmentation → TrOCR → Post-processing → JSON

Script :
  Here is our complete pipeline. Step by step.

  We take a prescription photo — JPG, PNG, PDF, whatever.
  We clean it: denoising, binarization, deskewing.
  We cut out each handwritten line individually.
  We run each line through TrOCR.
  And we reassemble everything into a clean, structured, usable JSON.

  [Pause]

  From visual chaos… to data. In under two seconds.

SLIDE 7 — Why TrOCR
----------------------------------------------------------------

Slide Title : "Why TrOCR? Three solid reasons."

Visual : Three blocks:
         [Brain] Pretrained LM · [Pen] Cursive-familiar · [HF logo] HuggingFace ecosystem

Script :
  Why this model and not another?

  TrOCR is an encoder-decoder architecture.
  The visual encoder is BEiT — it understands images as visual patches.
  The decoder is RoBERTa — it generates text like a real language model.

  Result: the model doesn't guess letter by letter.
  It understands words, context.

  And the trocr-base-handwritten checkpoint was already pre-trained
  on cursive handwriting. We're not starting from scratch.

  [Pause]

  We could spend our entire budget on data and fine-tuning.
  Not on architecture engineering.

SLIDE 9 — Dataset & Augmentation
----------------------------------------------------------------

Slide Title : "The data: our foundation."

Visual : Sample line crops with their ground-truth transcriptions.

Script :
  We worked with the chinmays18 dataset —
  publicly available on Hugging Face Hub.

  Around 5,000 line crops.
  Each line has its exact transcription:
  patient, drug, dose, frequency.

  Classic split: 80% training, 10% validation, 10% test.

  [Pause]

  But 5,000 samples is small.
  So we augmented artificially:
  slight rotation, blur, contrast jitter, elastic distortion.

  We tripled the effective training diversity.
  Without adding a single real prescription.

SLIDE 10 — Fine-tuning & Results
----------------------------------------------------------------

Slide Title : "From 78% to 4% error rate. The numbers speak."

Visual : Before/after table:
         CER    78%  →   4%
         WER    92%  →  11%
         Exact   3%  →  87%

Script :
  Here's the moment we've all been waiting for.

  Before fine-tuning — the base model, no adaptation —
  CER of 78%. Meaning: almost every character is wrong.
  Exact match? 3%. The machine recognizes almost nothing correctly.

  [Pause]

  After 30 epochs of fine-tuning on our medical data…

  CER: 4%.
  Exact match: 87%.

  [Pause — Let the numbers sink in]

  Even the latency stays reasonable:
  1.6 seconds per prescription on GPU.

  This is not theory.
  These are real results on our held-out test set.
